Psalm 62

1 For the music director, Jeduthun; a psalm of David. For God alone I patiently wait;2tn Heb “only for God [is] there silence [to] my soul.”
 he is the one who delivers me.3tn Heb “from him [is] my deliverance.”

2 He alone is my protector4tn Heb “my high rocky summit.”
 and deliverer. He is my refuge;5tn Or “my elevated place” (see Ps 18:2).
 I will not be upended.6tn The Hebrew text adds רַבָּה (rabbah, “greatly”) at the end of the line. It is unusual for this adverb to follow a negated verb. Some see this as qualifying the assertion to some degree, but this would water down the affirmation too much (see v. 6b, where the adverb is omitted). If the adverb has a qualifying function, it would suggest that the psalmist might be upended, though not severely. This is inconsistent with the confident mood of the psalm. The adverb probably has an emphatic force here, “I will not be greatly upended” meaning “I will not be annihilated.”
 3 How long will you threaten7tn The verb form is plural; the psalmist addresses his enemies. The verb הוּת occurs only here in the OT. An Arabic cognate means “shout at.”
 a man? All of you are murderers,8tn The Hebrew text has a Pual (passive) form, but the verb form should be vocalized as a Piel (active) form. See BDB 953-54 s.v. רָצַח.
 as dangerous as a leaning wall or an unstable fence.9tn Heb “like a bent wall and a broken fence.” The point of the comparison is not entirely clear. Perhaps the enemies are depicted as dangerous, like a leaning wall or broken fence that is in danger of falling on someone (see C. A. Briggs and E. G. Briggs, Psalms [ICC], 2:69).
 4 They10tn That is, the psalmist’s enemies addressed in the previous verse.
 spend all their time planning how to bring him11tn That is, the generic “man” referred to in the previous verse.
 down.12tn Heb “only from his lofty place [or perhaps, “dignity”] they plan to drive [him] away.”
 They love to use deceit;13tn Heb “they delight [in] a lie.”
 they pronounce blessings with their mouths, but inwardly they utter curses.14sn The enemies use deceit to bring down their victim. They make him think they are his friends by pronouncing blessings upon him, but inwardly they desire his demise.
 (Selah) 5 Patiently wait for God alone, my soul!15tn Heb “only for God be silent, my soul.” The wording is similar to that of v. 1a. Here an imperatival form, דּוֹמִּי (dommiy, “be silent”), appears instead of the noun דּוּמִיָּה (dumiyyah, “silence”). The psalmist is encouraging himself to maintain his trust in God.
 For he is the one who gives me confidence.16tn Heb “for from him [is] my hope.”
 6 He alone is my protector17tn Heb “my high rocky summit.”
 and deliverer. He is my refuge;18tn Or “my elevated place” (see Ps 18:2).
 I will not be upended.19sn The wording is identical to that of v. 2, except that רַבָּה (rabbah, “greatly”) does not appear in v. 6.
 7  God delivers me and exalts me; God is my strong protector and my shelter.20tn Heb “upon God [is] my deliverance and my glory, the high rocky summit of my strength, my shelter [is] in God.”
 8 Trust in him at all times, you people! Pour out your hearts before him!21tn To “pour out one’s heart” means to offer up to God intense, emotional lamentation and petitionary prayers (see Lam 2:19).
 God is our shelter! (Selah) 9 Men are nothing but a mere breath; human beings are unreliable.22tn Heb “only a breath [are] the sons of mankind, a lie [are] the sons of man.” The phrases “sons of mankind” and “sons of man” also appear together in Ps 49:2. Because of the parallel line there, where “rich and poor” are mentioned, a number of interpreters and translators treat these expressions as polar opposites, בְּנֵי אָדָם (bÿney ’adam) referring to the lower classes and בְּנֵי אִישׁ (bÿney ’ish) to higher classes. But usage does not support such a view. The rare phrase בְּנֵי אִישׁ (“sons of man”) appears to refer to human beings in general in its other uses (see Pss 4:2; Lam 3:33). It is better to understand the phrases as synonymous expressions.
 When they are weighed in the scales, all of them together are lighter than air.23tn The noun הֶבֶל (hevel), translated “a breath” earlier in the verse, appears again here.
 10 Do not trust in what you can gain by oppression!24tn Heb “do not trust in oppression.” Here “oppression” stands by metonymy for the riches that can be gained by oppressive measures, as the final line of the verse indicates.
 Do not put false confidence in what you can gain by robbery!25tn Heb “and in robbery do not place vain hope.” Here “robbery” stands by metonymy for the riches that can be gained by theft, as the next line of the verse indicates.
 If wealth increases, do not become attached to it!26tn Heb “[as for] wealth, when it bears fruit, do not set [your] heart [on it].”
 11 God has declared one principle; two principles I have heard:27tn Heb “one God spoke, two which I heard.” This is a numerical saying utilizing the “x” followed by “x + 1” pattern to facilitate poetic parallelism. (See W. M. W. Roth, Numerical Sayings in the Old Testament [VTSup], 55-56.) As is typical in such sayings, a list corresponding to the second number (in this case “two”) follows. Another option is to translate, “God has spoken once, twice [he has spoken] that which I have heard.” The terms אַחַת (’akhat, “one; once”) and שְׁתַּיִם (shÿtayim, “two; twice”) are also juxtaposed in 2 Kgs 6:10 (where they refer to an action that was done more than “once or twice”) and in Job 33:14 (where they refer to God speaking “one way” and then in “another manner”).
 God is strong,28tn Heb “that strength [belongs] to God.”
 12 and you, O Lord, demonstrate loyal love.29tn Heb “and to you, O Master, [is] loyal love.”
 For you repay men for what they do.30tn Heb “for you pay back to a man according to his deed.” Another option is to understand vv. 11b and 12a as the first principle and v. 12b as the second. In this case one might translate, “God has declared one principle, two principles I have heard, namely, that God is strong, and you, O Lord, demonstrate loyal love, and that you repay men for what they do.”


sn You repay men for what they do. The psalmist views God’s justice as a demonstration of both his power (see v. 11c) and his loyal love (see v. 12a). When God judges evildoers, he demonstrates loyal love to his people.
