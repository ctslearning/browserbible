Psalm 4

1 For the music director, to be accompanied by stringed instruments; a psalm of David. When I call out, answer me, O God who vindicates me!2tn Heb “God of my righteousness.”
 Though I am hemmed in, you will lead me into a wide, open place.3tn Heb “in distress (or “a narrow place”) you make (a place) large for me.” The function of the Hebrew perfect verbal form here is uncertain. The translation above assumes that the psalmist is expressing his certitude and confidence that God will intervene. The psalmist is so confident of God’s positive response to his prayer, he can describe God’s deliverance as if it had already happened. Such confidence is consistent with the mood of the psalm (vv. 3, 8). Another option is to take the perfects as precative, expressing a wish or request (“lead me”). See IBHS 494-95 §30.5.4c, d. However, not all grammarians are convinced that the perfect is used as a precative in biblical Hebrew.
 Have mercy on me4tn Or “show me favor.”
 and respond to5tn Heb “hear.”

2 You men,6tn Heb “sons of man.”
 how long will you try to turn my honor into shame?7tn Heb “how long my honor to shame?”
 How long8tn The interrogative construction עַד־מֶה (’ad-meh, “how long?”), is understood by ellipsis in the second line.
 will you love what is worthless9tn Heb “emptiness.”
 and search for what is deceptive?10tn Heb “a lie.” Some see the metonymic language of v. 2b (“emptiness, lie”) as referring to idols or false gods. However, there is no solid immediate contextual evidence for such an interpretation. It is more likely that the psalmist addresses those who threaten him (see v. 1) and refers in a general way to their sinful lifestyle. (See R. Mosis, TDOT 7:121.) The two terms allude to the fact that sinful behavior is ultimately fruitless and self-destructive.
 (Selah) 3 Realize that11tn Heb “and know that.”
 the Lord shows the godly special favor;12tn Heb “that the Lord sets apart a faithful one for himself.” The psalmist states a general principle, though the singular form and the parallel line indicate he has himself in mind as the representative godly person. A חָסִיד (khasid; here translated as “the godly”) is one who does what is right in God’s eyes and remains faithful to God (see Pss 12:1; 18:25; 31:23; 37:28; 86:2; 97:10).
 the Lord responds13tn Heb “hears.”
 when I cry out to him. 4 Tremble with fear and do not sin!14sn The psalmist warns his enemies that they need to tremble with fear before God and repudiate their sinful ways.
 Meditate as you lie in bed, and repent of your ways!15tn Heb “say in your heart(s) on your bed(s) and wail/lament.” The verb דֹמּוּ (dommu) is understood as a form of דָמָם (“wail, lament”) in sorrow and repentance. Another option is to take the verb from II דָמָם (damam, “be quiet”); cf. NIV, NRSV “be silent.”
 (Selah) 5 Offer the prescribed sacrifices16tn Or “proper, right.” The phrase also occurs in Deut 33:19 and Ps 51:19.
 and trust in the Lord!17sn Trust in the Lord. The psalmist urges his enemies to make peace with God and become his followers.
 6 Many say, “Who can show us anything good?” Smile upon us, Lord!18tn Heb “lift up upon us the light of your face, Lord.” The verb נסה is apparently an alternate form of נשׂא, “lift up.” See GKC 217 §76.b. The idiom “light of your face” probably refers to a smile (see Eccl 8:1), which in turn suggests favor and blessing (see Num 6:25; Pss 31:16; 44:3; 67:1; 80:3, 7, 19; 89:15; Dan 9:17).


sn Smile upon us. Though many are discouraged, the psalmist asks the Lord to intervene and transform the situation.

 than those who have abundant grain and wine.20tn Heb “from (i.e., more than) the time (when) their grain and their wine are abundant.”
 8 I will lie down and sleep peacefully,21tn Heb “in peace at the same time I will lie down and sleep.”
 for you, Lord, make me safe and secure.22tn Heb “for you, Lord, solitarily, securely make me dwell.” The translation understands לְבָדָד (lÿvadad) as modifying the verb; the Lord keeps enemies away from the psalmist so that he is safe and secure. Another option is to take לְבָדָד with what precedes and translate, “you alone, Lord, make me secure.”
