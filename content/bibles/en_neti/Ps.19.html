Psalm 19

1 For the music director; a psalm of David. The heavens declare the glory of God;2sn God’s glory refers here to his royal majesty and power.
 the sky displays his handiwork.3tn Heb “and the work of his hands the sky declares.” The participles emphasize the ongoing testimony of the heavens/sky.

2 Day after day it speaks out;4tn Heb “it gushes forth a word.” The “sky” (see v. 1b) is the subject of the verb. Though not literally speaking (see v. 3), it clearly reveals God’s royal majesty. The sun’s splendor and its movement across the sky is in view (see vv. 4-6).
 night after night it reveals his greatness.5tn Heb “it [i.e., the sky] declares knowledge,” i.e., knowledge about God’s royal majesty and power (see v. 1). This apparently refers to the splendor and movements of the stars. The imperfect verbal forms in v. 2, like the participles in the preceding verse, combine with the temporal phrases (“day after day” and “night after night”) to emphasize the ongoing testimony of the sky.
 3 There is no actual speech or word, nor is its6tn Heb “their.” The antecedent of the plural pronoun is “heavens” (v. 1).
 voice literally heard. 4 Yet its voice7tc The MT reads, “their measuring line” (קוּם, qum). The noun קַו (qav, “measuring line”) makes no sense in this context. The reading קוֹלָם (qolam, “their voice”) which is supported by the LXX, is preferable.
 echoes8tn Heb “goes out,” or “proceeds forth.”
 throughout the earth; its9tn Heb “their” (see the note on the word “its” in v. 3).
 words carry10tn The verb is supplied in the translation. The Hebrew text has no verb; יָצָא (yatsa’, “goes out”) is understood by ellipsis.
 to the distant horizon.11tn Heb “to the end of the world.”
 In the sky12tn Heb “in them” (i.e., the heavens).
 he has pitched a tent for the sun.13sn He has pitched a tent for the sun. The personified sun emerges from this “tent” in order to make its daytime journey across the sky. So the “tent” must refer metaphorically to the place where the sun goes to rest during the night.
 5 Like a bridegroom it emerges14tn The participle expresses the repeated or regular nature of the action.
 from its chamber;15tn The Hebrew noun חֻפָּה (khufah, “chamber”) occurs elsewhere only in Isa 4:5 and Joel 2:16 (where it refers to the bedroom of a bride and groom).


sn Like a bridegroom. The metaphor likens the sun to a bridegroom who rejoices on his wedding night.
 like a strong man it enjoys16tn The imperfect verbal form draws attention to the regularity of the action.
 running its course.17tn Heb “[on] a path.”


sn Like a strong man. The metaphorical language reflects the brilliance of the sunrise, which attests to the sun’s vigor.
 6 It emerges from the distant horizon,18tn Heb “from the end of the heavens [is] its going forth.”
 and goes from one end of the sky to the other;19tn Heb “and its circuit [is] to their ends.”
 nothing can escape20tn Heb “is hidden from.”
 its heat. 7 The law of the Lord is perfect and preserves one’s life.21tn Heb “[it] restores life.” Elsewhere the Hiphil of שׁוּב (shuv, “return”) when used with נֶפֶשׁ (nefesh, “life”) as object, means to “rescue or preserve one’s life” (Job 33:30; Ps 35:17) or to “revive one’s strength” (emotionally or physically; cf. Ruth 4:15; Lam 1:11, 16, 19). Here the point seems to be that the law preserves the life of the one who studies it by making known God’s will. Those who know God’s will know how to please him and can avoid offending him. See v. 11a.
 The rules set down by the Lord22tn Traditionally, “the testimony of the Lord.” The noun עֵדוּת (’edut) refers here to the demands of God’s covenant law.
 are reliable23tn God’s covenant contains a clear, reliable witness to his moral character and demands.
 and impart wisdom to the inexperienced.24tn Or “the [morally] naive,” that is, the one who is young and still in the process of learning right from wrong and distinguishing wisdom from folly.
 8 The Lord’s precepts are fair25tn Or “just.” Perhaps the idea is that they impart a knowledge of what is just and right.
 and make one joyful.26tn Heb “[they] make happy [the] heart.” Perhaps the point is that they bring a sense of joyful satisfaction to the one who knows and keeps them, for those who obey God’s law are richly rewarded. See v. 11b.
 The Lord’s commands27tn Heb “command.” The singular here refers to the law as a whole.
 are pure28tn Because they reflect God’s character, his commands provide a code of moral and ethical purity.
 and give insight for life.29tn Heb [they] enlighten [the] eyes.
 9 The commands to fear the Lord are right30tn Heb “the fear of the Lord is clean.” The phrase “fear of the Lord” probably refers here to the law, which teaches one how to demonstrate proper reverence for the Lord. See Ps 111:10 for another possible use of the phrase in this sense.
 and endure forever.31tn Heb “[it] stands permanently.”
 The judgments given by the Lord are trustworthy and absolutely just.32sn Trustworthy and absolutely just. The Lord’s commands accurately reflect God’s moral will for his people and are an expression of his just character.
 10 They are of greater value33tn Heb “more desirable.”

 12 Who can know all his errors?37tn Heb “Errors who can discern?” This rhetorical question makes the point that perfect moral discernment is impossible to achieve. Consequently it is inevitable that even those with good intentions will sin on occasion.
 Please do not punish me for sins I am unaware of.38tn Heb “declare me innocent from hidden [things],” i.e., sins. In this context (see the preceding line) “hidden” sins are not sins committed in secret, but sins which are not recognized as such by the psalmist.

Another option is to translate the form as an imperfect continuing the thought of v. 14b: “[Then] the words of my mouth and the thought of my heart will be acceptable before you.”
 O Lord, my sheltering rock43tn Heb “my rocky cliff,” which is a metaphor for protection; thus the translation “sheltering rock.”
 and my redeemer.44tn Heb “and the one who redeems me.” The metaphor casts the Lord in the role of a leader who protects members of his extended family in times of need and crisis.
